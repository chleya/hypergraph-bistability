# Abstract

How does structural order emerge in complex systems? Classical theories attribute order formation to entropy reduction, energy minimization, or the transport and accumulation of components. Here we present evidence for an alternative mechanism: **structural order emerges via in-situ reconstruction of complexity rather than transport**.

We study a minimal hypergraph dynamics model with local rules (growth, fusion, split, deletion) and a capacity constraint k. We discover a phase transition at k_c ≈ 2.35 where the system exhibits bistability between fragmented and clustered states. More importantly, we observe a striking **order-complexity separation**: as the order parameter M increases, the structural diversity H of the core region increases while that of the peripheral region decreases, with strong correlations (r = ±0.97).

To understand the mechanism, we conduct three critical experiments. First, flow-blocking experiments reveal that fusion events are negligible (≈0), indicating no transport from periphery to core. Second, identity tracking shows that 75.8% of core structures are **newly generated** during evolution, while only 24.2% are inherited from early stages. Third, perturbation tests confirm that this behavior is structurally invariant across different rule implementations.

These findings lead to a **Complexity Reconstruction Principle**: in non-thermal systems with capacity constraints, structural complexity is not conserved and transported but continuously regenerated through local reorganization. This mechanism explains why order and complexity can increase simultaneously in the core while decreasing in the periphery—a phenomenon fundamentally different from entropy-driven phase transitions.

Our results suggest that structure formation in networks, multi-agent systems, and potentially neural representations may arise from reconstruction dynamics rather than accumulation, opening new perspectives for understanding emergent organization in complex systems.
