# Introduction

## The Problem of Emergent Order

How does structural order emerge from disordered components? This fundamental question underlies our understanding of formation in physical, biological, and social systems.

## Classical Paradigms

Three dominant frameworks have shaped the field:

1. **Entropy reduction**: Ordered structures form by decreasing entropy, as formalized in statistical mechanics and Landau theory.

2. **Energy minimization**: Systems relax to energy minima, as in spin glasses and protein folding.

3. **Transport and accumulation**: Components aggregate or accumulate to form structures, as in crystallization or network growth models.

These frameworks share a common assumption: **complexity is conserved and transported**.

## Our Discovery

Here we present evidence for an alternative mechanism. Using a minimal hypergraph dynamics model with local rules and capacity constraints, we discover that:

- A phase transition occurs at k_c ≈ 2.35, exhibiting bistability
- **Order-complexity separation**: as order increases, complexity increases in the core but decreases in the periphery
- **No transport mechanism**: fusion events are negligible, and flow-blocking has no effect
- **In-situ reconstruction**: 75.8% of core structures are newly generated, not inherited

These findings reveal **Complexity Reconstruction** as a distinct principle: structural complexity can be regenerated in place rather than accumulated or transported.

## Contributions

1. Discovery of a phase transition in capacity-constrained hypergraph dynamics
2. Demonstration of order-complexity separation as a new phenomenon
3. Evidence that complexity reconstruction—not transport—drives structure formation
4. A structural invariance that holds across different rule implementations

This work challenges the transport/accumulation assumption and suggests that reconstruction dynamics may underlie emergent organization in diverse complex systems.
