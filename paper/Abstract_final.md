# Abstract (Final Version - 192 words)

The emergence of structured order in interacting systems is commonly attributed to entropy reduction, energy minimization, or the transport and accumulation of structural complexity. Here, we study a minimal hypergraph system governed by simple local rules, in which ordered cores spontaneously emerge from disordered initial conditions. We observe a robust order–complexity separation: as global order increases, structural complexity concentrates in the core while the periphery becomes progressively simplified. Crucially, this process cannot be explained by transport. Fusion events are negligible, and no measurable periphery-to-core flow is detected. Instead, rigorous identity tracking with parent chain tracing shows that the majority (96.4% ± 8.6%) of core structures are newly generated during evolution, with only a minor fraction (0.4 ± 0.8 fusion events per run) originating from fusion. Together, these results rule out accumulation-based explanations and demonstrate that structural order arises through in-situ reconstruction of complexity. This identifies a distinct organizing principle for non-thermal systems, in which order emerges from continuous structural reconfiguration rather than entropy reduction.
